Geotechnical analysis for soft soil tunnels

The contrast between the alluvial fans of Summerlin and the clay-rich flats near the Las Vegas Wash tells the story of this valley's subsurface complexity. In Summerlin, gravelly sands drain quickly and remain largely unsaturated, while near the Wash, silty clays undergo dramatic volume changes with moisture shifts. Unsaturated soil analysis in Las Vegas is essential because the region's arid climate keeps the water table deep — often over 200 feet — yet episodic monsoon rains and landscape irrigation can trigger sudden wetting fronts. Understanding soil suction, collapse potential, and swelling behavior through controlled laboratory testing prevents the kind of foundation heave or differential settlement that plagues improperly designed slabs. Before finalizing footing design, teams often pair this analysis with a permeability field test to capture how infiltration rates vary across the site's layered deposits.

In Las Vegas, the primary risk is not a high water table — it is the sudden, uneven wetting of unsaturated soils that triggers differential settlement.

Service characteristics in Las Vegas

Southern Nevada's extreme evapotranspiration rates — among the highest in the continental U.S. — mean that near-surface soils spend most of the year in a desiccated, high-suction state. Yet when water is introduced, either by broken utility lines or overwatering of xeriscaped lots, those same soils can lose strength rapidly. The key parameters measured in an unsaturated soil analysis include matric suction via filter paper or tensiometer methods, the soil-water characteristic curve (SWCC), and collapse index under controlled wetting. The local geology, dominated by Quaternary alluvium and lacustrine deposits from the ancient Lake Manly, produces a wide range of soil types within a single parcel. For projects that involve cut slopes or retaining walls, the same laboratory data can feed directly into a stability analysis for slopes that accounts for the loss of apparent cohesion upon saturation.

Critical ground factors in Las Vegas

The laboratory technician places a carefully trimmed specimen into a fixed-ring oedometer cell, then applies a seating load before flooding the sample with deionized water. This simple action — wetting a dry specimen — replicates what happens when a burst irrigation line saturates the ground beneath a residential slab in Green Valley or Henderson. The dial gauge measures vertical displacement over hours and days, revealing whether the soil will collapse, swell, or remain stable. In Las Vegas, the most common outcome is collapse: a metastable soil structure, held together by suction bonds, that disintegrates upon wetting. A collapse strain above 1% warrants deeper foundation elements or ground improvement. Without this data, a contractor risks placing a slab on a surface that will drop several centimeters after the first heavy rain or landscaping cycle.

Our services

Our Las Vegas geotechnical laboratory offers two specialized services focused on the behavior of partially saturated soils.

Soil-Water Characteristic Curve Testing

Full SWCC determination using pressure plate, chilled-mirror hygrometer, and filter paper methods. Covers suction ranges from 0 to 150 MPa for design of cover systems, slope stability in arid fills, and foundation moisture buffers.

Collapse and Swell Potential Assessment

One-dimensional oedometer tests with controlled wetting under multiple surcharge loads. Reports collapse index, swelling pressure, and free swell for each soil horizon. Directly applicable to slab-on-grade and shallow foundation design in Las Vegas alluvium.
